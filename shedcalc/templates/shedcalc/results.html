Considerations

Sampling Strategy

The accuracy of SH exposure ages is dependent on the sampling strategy involved. Users should follow existing guidelines for using the Schmidt Hammer 1. However, there is currently no established guidelines with regards to the number of R-Values per surface2,3,4. The minimum sample size varies significantly depending on lithology4, ranging from just 6 R-Values (Feldspathic sandstone, α = 0·05) to >39 (Gabbro, Serpentinite, Greenstone, Conglomerate)3. Given the sensitivity of the Schmidt Hammer to surface discontinuities, moisture content and lichen, a sufficiently large dataset is necessary, typically >25 R-Values for weak and moderately strong rocks5. In our projects 4,6, we have collected 30 R-Values per surface as this is suitable for the medium to coarse-grained granite boulder and bedrock surfaces which underpin our calibration curve5. In addition, there is also uncertainty with regards to the number of surfaces per site. SHED-publications in the Mourne Mountains, Northern Ireland and Cumbria, NW England have sampled between 37 and 304 surfaces per site. This is ultimately dependent on the scale of your research project, whether it wants to constrain the de-glacial chronology of an entire mountain range 7 or a single glacial landform4, and also what is feasible within time/financial limitations.

Geomorphological Setting

While a robust sampling strategy and large geochronological datasets can provide an important insight into past events, it is necessary to consider the geomorphological and morphostratigraphic context of the dated landform or deposit 8. SH exposure ages relate to the emplacement or exhumation of surfaces and the onset of aerial weathering processes and thus may only provide a limiting age in some scenarios i.e. “this event occurred no later than X thousand years ago”. In many geomorphological settings, the onset of weathering may be contemporaneous with the dated event e.g. glacial retreat, but this is not necessarily the case.

Statistical Analysis

Having carefully considered the geomorphological setting of your dated landform, the final crucial stage is to undertake robust statistical analysis. Although we cannot discuss in detail the wealth of analytical tools available, we can advise on the techniques we use. We recommend taking the arithmetic mean after excluding obvious outliers to provide a suitable exposure age for your landform. Since most data sets are not perfectly normally distributed, we recommend using the mean absolute deviation to estimate uncertainty. The mean absolute deviation deals with skewness and minor outliers in a more robust way than standard deviation. If your dataset is of sufficient size (~30 samples), the mean absolute deviation can be of comparable accuracy and precision to terrestrial cosmogenic nuclide ages4. For more robust analysis, probably density estimates or Gaussian modelling7,9 can be used to eliminate positive or negative skew. For advice on Gaussian modelling, please contact .
